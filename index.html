Artemis II

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Artemis II is NASA's first crewed mission under the Artemis program, and the first crewed flight of the Space Launch System (SLS) rocket and Orion spacecraft.[1] The mission launched on April 1, 2026 from Launch Complex 39B at Kennedy Space Center, Florida, sending four astronauts on a 10-day journey around the Moon and back — the first crewed mission to travel to lunar distance since Apollo 17 in 1972. [2]

The mission's primary objectives include testing the Orion spacecraft's life support systems with crew aboard for the first time, and laying the groundwork for future crewed Artemis missions including Artemis III, which aims to land humans on the lunar surface. [3]

Introduction [edit]

Artemis II is part of NASA's broader Artemis program, which aims to return humans to the Moon for the first time since the Apollo era and eventually establish a sustainable human presence on and around the Moon as a stepping stone to Mars.[1] The mission follows the successful uncrewed Artemis I test flight launched on November 16, 2022, which validated the SLS rocket and Orion spacecraft systems.

Unlike Artemis III, no lunar landing takes place on Artemis II. Instead the crew performs a free-return trajectory around the Moon, testing all critical systems needed for future missions.[4]

Crew [edit]

Artemis II carries a crew of four astronauts representing NASA and the Canadian Space Agency (CSA).[2]

Commander

Reid Wiseman serves as Commander of Artemis II. A NASA astronaut and former Chief of the Astronaut Office, Wiseman previously flew aboard the International Space Station during Expedition 40/41 in 2014.

Mission Specialists

  • Victor Glover — Pilot. NASA astronaut and Navy test pilot, Glover previously served aboard the ISS in 2020–2021 and became the first Black astronaut to live and work on the station long-term. [5]
  • Christina Koch — Mission Specialist. NASA astronaut who holds the record for the longest single spaceflight by a woman (328 days). She is the first woman to travel to lunar distance. [5]
  • Jeremy Hansen — Mission Specialist. Canadian Space Agency astronaut and the first Canadian to travel beyond low Earth orbit. [6]

Background & History [edit]

Artemis I (2022)

The Artemis program began with the uncrewed Artemis I mission, launched on November 16, 2022. Artemis I sent an uncrewed Orion spacecraft on a 25-day mission around the Moon and back, validating the SLS and Orion systems before committing crew to flight.[1] The mission was a success and cleared the path for the crewed Artemis II.

Preparation for Artemis II

Following Artemis I, NASA and its partners spent years preparing for the crewed mission. The fully stacked SLS vehicle with Orion was rolled out to Launch Pad 39B at Kennedy Space Center on January 17, 2026 — a journey of four miles taking approximately 12 hours.[7] A wet dress rehearsal followed, though it faced minor delays due to cold weather and a liquid hydrogen leak before being successfully completed.

Mission Profile [edit]

Artemis II launched on April 1, 2026 at 6:35 PM EDT from Launch Complex 39B. [2] The mission follows a free-return trajectory that takes the crew around the Moon over approximately 10 days before returning to Earth. Key activities during the mission include:

  • Testing Orion's life support systems with crew aboard for the first time.
  • Manual piloting tests of the Orion spacecraft.
  • Radiation shelter construction demonstration.
  • Three trajectory correction burns for precise return to Earth.
  • Testing communication systems at lunar distance.

The crew relies on Orion's European Service Module (ESM), provided by the European Space Agency (ESA), for power, water, air, and thrust throughout the mission.[4]

Spacecraft [edit]

The Orion spacecraft is NASA's next-generation crew vehicle designed for deep space exploration.[3] It consists of two main modules:

  • Crew Module (CM): Built by Lockheed Martin, it carries the four crew members and serves as their living quarters during the mission.
  • European Service Module (ESM): Provided by ESA and based on the Automated Transfer Vehicle, it provides propulsion, power, water, and breathable air to the crew.

The Space Launch System (SLS) is the most powerful rocket NASA has ever built, generating 8.8 million pounds of thrust at liftoff — surpassing even the Saturn V used during the Apollo program. [7]

Significance [edit]

Artemis II marks humanity's return to lunar distance for the first time in over 50 years. It is historically significant for several reasons: [6]

  • First crewed mission to travel to the Moon since Apollo 17 in December 1972.
  • First woman — Christina Koch — to travel to lunar distance.
  • First person of colour — Victor Glover — to travel to lunar distance.
  • First Canadian — Jeremy Hansen — to travel beyond low Earth orbit.
  • First crewed flight of the SLS rocket and Orion spacecraft.

NASA targets early 2028 for the first Artemis lunar landing (Artemis III), which will bring humans back to the surface of the Moon for the first time since 1972.[8]
